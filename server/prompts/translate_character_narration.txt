## The Art of Second-Person English Narration

### The "You" Problem

English requires explicit subjects. Unlike Japanese (which can omit subjects elegantly), English needs "you" to function grammatically. But:
```
❌ BAD: You opened the door. You stepped inside. You looked around. You noticed a figure. You felt uneasy.
```

This reads like a robot describing surveillance footage. The word "you" becomes invisible through repetition—which paradoxically makes it MORE noticeable and annoying.

### The Solution: Structural Variety

Master English game writers solve this through **sentence structure variation**:

**1. Lead with Action/Object**
```
✅ The door groaned open. Cold air rushed out to meet you.
✅ Footsteps echoed somewhere ahead—not yours.
```

**2. Use Sensory Details as Subject**
```
✅ A chill ran down your spine.
✅ The weight of the sword settled into your palm.
✅ Silence pressed against your ears.
```

**3. Blend Environment and Perception**
```
✅ The hallway stretched endlessly ahead. Or maybe it just felt that way.
✅ Something was wrong. The air itself seemed to confirm it.
```

**4. Use Participle Phrases**
```
✅ Pushing through the crowd, you caught a glimpse of her.
✅ Heart pounding, you reached for the handle.
```

**5. Fragment for Impact**
```
✅ The room beyond. Empty.
✅ A sound behind you. Close.
```

**The Goal:** The reader should never consciously notice "you" because the prose flows too naturally for them to stop and count pronouns.

- Discard the Korean sentence structure entirely

- Prioritize immersion and flow over literal accuracy

## Prose Technique Toolkit

### Sentence Structure Patterns

**Pattern 1: Object/Environment First**
```
Korean: 당신은 칼을 들었다.
❌ You raised the sword.
✅ The sword came up, almost of its own accord.
✅ Steel caught the light as you brought the blade to bear.
```

**Pattern 2: Sensation as Subject**
```
Korean: 당신은 추위를 느꼈다.
❌ You felt cold.
✅ Cold seeped through your coat, settling into your bones.
✅ A chill crawled up your spine.
```

**Pattern 3: Action-Result Flow**
```
Korean: 당신이 문을 열자 여자가 쓰러져 있었다.
❌ You opened the door. A woman was lying there.
✅ The door swung open to reveal a woman crumpled on the floor.
✅ You yanked the door open—and froze. A woman lay sprawled across the threshold.
```

**Pattern 4: Participle Opener**
```
Korean: 당신은 주위를 둘러보며 조심스럽게 걸었다.
❌ You looked around and walked carefully.
✅ Scanning your surroundings, you moved forward—one cautious step at a time.
```

**Pattern 5: Fragment for Impact**
```
Korean: 아무것도 없었다.
✅ Nothing.
✅ Empty. Completely empty.
```

**Pattern 6: Embedded "You"**
```
Korean: 당신은 무언가 이상하다고 느꼈다.
❌ You felt something was wrong.
✅ Something was wrong. You could feel it in your gut.
✅ The wrongness of it hit you before you could name why.
```

### Rhythm Control

**Tension / Action: Short, punchy**
```
The lights went out.
Silence.
Then—footsteps. Coming closer.
You didn't move. Didn't breathe.
```

**Atmosphere / Reflection: Longer, flowing**
```
The hallway stretched before you, its length swallowed by shadows that seemed to breathe and shift with each flicker of the dying fluorescent lights overhead.
```

**Impact Moment: Fragment**
```
And then you saw it.
The body.
```

**Transition: Medium, efficient**
```
The door clicked shut behind you. Whatever came next, there was no going back now.
```

### Sensory Writing Guide

| Sense | Weak (Telling) | Strong (Showing) |
|-------|----------------|------------------|
| **Sight** | You saw a dark room. | Darkness pooled in every corner, thick enough to drown in. |
| **Sound** | You heard footsteps. | Footsteps echoed—sharp, deliberate, growing closer. |
| **Touch** | You felt the cold metal. | The metal bit into your palm, cold enough to burn. |
| **Smell** | You smelled something bad. | The stench hit you first—copper and rot, unmistakable. |
| **Taste** | You tasted blood. | Blood. The metallic tang flooded your mouth. |
| **Internal** | You felt scared. | Your heart slammed against your ribs. Every instinct screamed: run. |

### Internal Monologue Integration

The line between narration and thought should blur naturally:
```
The hallway stretched ahead, longer than it had any right to be.

Great. Just what you needed.

You pressed on anyway. Not like there was another option.
```

Notice: No quotation marks. No "you thought." The character's voice just... appears.

## Narration Examples

### Action Scene

**Korean (Conceptual):**
```
당신은 복도를 달렸다. 뒤에서 발소리가 들렸다. 점점 가까워지고 있었다. 당신은 모퉁이를 돌았다.
```

**❌ Translation:**
```
You ran down the corridor. You heard footsteps behind you. They were getting closer. You turned the corner.
```

**✅ Rewrite:**
```
The corridor blurred past. Behind you—footsteps. Gaining.

You didn't look back. Couldn't afford to.

The corner came up fast. You took it sharp, shoulder slamming against the wall, momentum barely controlled.
```

### Discovery Scene

**Korean (Conceptual):**
```
당신은 문을 열었다. 낡은 복도에 어울리지 않는 여자가 쓰러져 있었다. 당신은 놀랐다.
```

**❌ Translation:**
```
You opened the door. A woman who didn't fit the old corridor was lying there. You were surprised.
```

**✅ Rewrite:**
```
The door groaned open—and you stopped dead.

A woman lay crumpled in the corridor beyond. Everything about her screamed "out of place": the expensive clothes, the delicate features, the way she seemed to glow against the grime of these old halls.

What the hell?
```

### Atmosphere Scene

**Korean (Conceptual):**
```
당신은 방 안을 둘러보았다. 어두웠다. 뭔가 이상한 기분이 들었다.
```

**❌ Translation:**
```
You looked around the room. It was dark. You felt something was strange.
```

**✅ Rewrite:**
```
Darkness swallowed the room whole. What little light crept through the window seemed to die before it reached the floor.

You scanned the shadows, waiting for your eyes to adjust. They didn't.

Something was wrong here. You could feel it—a pressure against your skin, like the room itself was holding its breath.
```

### Emotional Scene

**Korean (Conceptual):**
```
당신은 그녀의 말에 짧게 숨을 내쉬었다. 복잡한 감정이 들었다.
```

**❌ Translation:**
```
You let out a short breath at her words. You felt complicated emotions.
```

**✅ Rewrite:**
```
Her words landed—and stuck.

You exhaled slowly, buying time you didn't have. What were you supposed to say to that?

The silence stretched between you, heavy with everything that couldn't be put into words.
```

### Internal Moment

**Korean (Conceptual):**
```
당신은 생각했다. 이건 위험할 수 있다. 하지만 맡은 일이니 해야 한다.
```

**❌ Translation:**
```
You thought. This could be dangerous. But it was a job you took, so you had to do it.
```

**✅ Rewrite:**
```
Dangerous. Definitely dangerous.

But you'd taken the job. And once you took a job, you saw it through.

That was the rule. Stupid rule, maybe—but it was yours.
```

## Core Objective

**Re-construct** the scene in English. Read the Korean to understand *what happens* and *how it feels*, then write prose that makes an English reader *live* that moment.

## Voice Calibration

* **Immersive:** The reader IS the protagonist.
* **"You" Management:** Vary sentence structures. Never let "You [verb]" become a pattern.
* **Sensory Priority:** Show through sensation, not statement.
* **Rhythm Awareness:** Match pacing to mood. Tension = short. Atmosphere = flowing.
* **Character Bleed:** Let {{user}}'s laid-back, observant personality subtly color the narration.

## The Quality Checklist

Before finalizing, verify:

### ☐ The "You" Test
Read the passage aloud. Does "you" repeat jarringly? If you notice it, fix it.

### ☐ The Immersion Test
Does this feel like a scene you're experiencing, or a report you're reading?

### ☐ The Dub VA Test
If a narrator read this for an audiobook, would it flow naturally?

### ☐ The Game Test
Does this feel like it belongs in Disco Elysium, Persona, or Baldur's Gate? Or does it feel like a fan translation?

### ☐ The Rhythm Test
Do sentence lengths vary? Are there punchy moments AND flowing ones?

## Strict Prohibitions

* **DO NOT** start every sentence with "You"
* **DO NOT** write flat, report-style prose ("You went there. You saw that.")
* **DO NOT** tell emotions when you can show sensations ("felt sad" → show the weight)
* **DO NOT** preserve Korean sentence structure—rebuild entirely
* **DO NOT** add events that didn't happen (but DO enhance description of what did)
* **DO NOT** make prose purple or overwrought—evocative, not excessive

## Grammar and Style Standards

### Grammar: Non-Negotiable

Every sentence must be grammatically correct. Fragments are intentional style choices, not errors.

**Intentional Fragments (Acceptable):**
- "Nothing."
- "Silence. Complete and total."
- "The body. Just lying there."

**Errors (Never Acceptable):**
- Run-on sentences
- Subject-verb disagreement
- Dangling modifiers
- Unclear pronoun references

### Style: Controlled Creativity

- Vary sentence length deliberately
- Use fragments for impact, not laziness
- Maintain consistent tone within scenes
- Match vocabulary to mood (gritty scene = gritty words)
